Last updated April 2026.* --- ## How to use this document This isn't meant to be read end-to-end and acted on uniformly. It's a menu. 1. **Skim Section 2 first.** Pick 2–3 paths that genuinely excite you, not ones that "make sense on paper." 2. **For each picked path:** read the role description, target companies, and "where to apply." Save the company links you actually want to pursue. 3. **Run the weekly action plan in Section 9 against your top 1–2 paths.** Don't spread across more than 2 simultaneously — you'll dilute your signal. 4. **Treat the "far-fetched" tier as a 6–12 month prep target,** not an immediate application target. A note on framing: your Rocketlane experience is a real asset. It's a respected SaaS brand, you've seen a product close-up, and you've worked cross-functionally. The transition isn't "starting over" — it's repositioning. --- ## Quick reference: difficulty & prep time | Tier | Definition | Realistic prep window from today | |---|---|---| | **Tier 1 — Strong fit** | Direct match for your background; can apply immediately or within 4–8 weeks | 1–2 months | | **Tier 2 — Stretch** | Reachable with focused upskilling and a portfolio project | 3–6 months | | **Tier 3 — Far-fetched** | Possible with sustained effort and a credible public body of work | 6–12 months | --- # Section 1: Pre-flight (do this regardless of which path you pick) Before the role-specific work, three things compound across every path on this list. They're worth one focused week before you start applying. **LinkedIn rebuild.** Headline format: `[Target role keywords] | ex-Rocketlane | building [thing you're learning]`. Recruiters search exact role titles — "Product Specialist," "Technical Account Manager," "AI Product," "Product Analyst" — and your headline needs to contain those words. The "Open to work" frame on your photo signals desperation; a clear public post saying what you're exploring signals intent. Use the post. **Resume rewrite around outcomes.** Three to four bullets per role, each starting with a verb and ending with a number or named outcome. "Resolved customer tickets" is invisible. "Built triage workflow that cut average resolution time by X% across Y customer segment" is hireable. Find your three real moments of impact at Rocketlane — a process you fixed, a customer you saved, a tool you built, an initiative you led — and make them the spine. **Public proof of one project.** The cafe billing system you mentioned is genuinely your highest-leverage asset right now. A shipped, working, public version of that — even at 70% polish — becomes your portfolio piece, your interview story, and your LinkedIn content for two months. With AI tooling, a working v1 is 4–6 weeks of evening work. This is the single most valuable thing you can spend time on while job-searching. --- # Section 2: Role categories ## Category A — Product roles (closest to your stated interest) ### A1. Be clear-eyed about your money. If you have 3+ months of savings, you can afford to be selective. If it's tighter, your real strategy is: take any solid Tier-1 offer (Product Specialist, Product Analyst, Implementation) within 6 weeks, then jump again in 12 months from a far stronger position. That second jump will be 10x easier than the current one. **On "managerial."** The "managerial" label can mean very different things. Most roles on this list are individual contributor (IC) roles with cross-functional ownership. Genuine people-management roles in tech almost always require 4+ years of IC experience first. Your "ownership and leading initiatives" instinct is what hiring managers in IC product roles want; explicit people-management is a 3+ year goal. **On "less customer-facing."** A few of the roles I've kept on this list (Product Specialist, DevRel, FDE) still involve some user/customer interaction — but the relationship is different from support. You're shaping the product they use, not unblocking their tickets. If "no customer interaction at all" is a hard constraint, prioritize: Product Analyst, Product Engineer, AI Engineer, Technical Writer, Design Engineer, Analytics Engineer, BizOps. These are genuinely behind-the-scenes. **On AI-related FOMO.** AI roles pay well right now and the field is genuinely exciting. But picking AI because it's hot, when your underlying interest is in building products generally, is a recipe for two years of feeling lost.
